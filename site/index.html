The United States is the sickest wealthy nation on earth by chronic disease metrics — a structural deterioration spanning decades that has now reached epidemic proportions across virtually every major health category. The CDC documents that 194 million American adults carry at least one chronic condition. The US has 2.4× the obesity rate of comparable wealthy nations, 2× the diabetes rate, 1.7× the asthma rate, and 1.3× the depression rate. These are not marginal differences. They are categorical ones that drive shorter life expectancy and higher death rates than every peer nation the US is typically compared against.

The food supply is where the physical health picture intersects most directly with institutional failure. The FDA's own Commissioner stated in July 2025: "The threats posed to our health by foods often considered ultra-processed are clear and convincing." HHS Secretary RFK Jr. stated simultaneously: "Ultra-processed foods are driving our chronic disease epidemic." These are not activist claims. They are the current official positions of the federal agencies responsible for the food supply — the same agencies that permitted the current state of affairs for decades.

Glyphosate — the world's most widely used herbicide and active ingredient in Roundup — is detected in 70–80% of all people examined including children, according to the University of Washington's Seattle Statement. Food is the primary route of exposure. Americans' glyphosate exposure increased approximately 500% since Roundup Ready GMO crops were introduced in 1996 (JAMA, 2017). The regulatory contradiction is explicit: the EPA classifies glyphosate as not likely to cause cancer. The IARC classifies it as "probably carcinogenic." These are not nuanced differences of degree. They are opposite conclusions from two authoritative bodies — one of which has jurisdiction over the American food supply.

The US experienced 700,000 excess deaths above baseline in 2023 — exactly what researchers at Boston University and the University of Minnesota predicted based on prior rising trends, entirely independent of the pandemic. They describe this as "a protracted health crisis for decades" driven by drug overdose, gun violence, and preventable cardiometabolic deaths. The US has been in a structural mortality crisis for forty years. COVID did not cause it. It revealed it.

It is the specific product of a system that treats health as a commodity rather than a common good, and that has successfully lobbied against every structural reform that would change this.

The Congressional Budget Office projects that the federal budget deficit in fiscal year 2026 is $1.9 trillion — 5.8% of GDP. The 50-year historical average for the deficit is 3.8% of GDP. The CBO projects deficits will exceed 5% of GDP every single year from 2020 to 2056 — something that has never occurred for more than five consecutive years in American history outside of World War II. This is not a projection of a future risk. It is the current reality extended forward under current law.

Net interest payments reached $970 billion in 2025 — tied with 1991 as the all-time high as a share of GDP. They are projected to exceed $1 trillion in fiscal year 2026 — nearly triple the $345 billion paid in 2020. Interest payments are currently the fastest-growing line in the federal budget. By 2038 they will exceed total discretionary spending — meaning the US government will spend more paying interest on past borrowing than on everything else it funds through discretionary spending combined.

The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget, analyzing CBO's February 2026 baseline, identified the debt spiral risk: the average interest rate paid on the national debt could exceed the economic growth rate starting in fiscal year 2031. When the interest rate on debt exceeds the growth rate of the economy, debt compounds faster than the economy can grow. This is a mathematically self-reinforcing dynamic from which recovery requires either significant revenue increases, significant spending cuts, or both — none of which either party has proposed at the required scale. Under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act incorporated into NBER projections, the debt-to-GDP ratio reaches 183% by 2054.

A civilization that does not replace itself is making a choice about its future — even if that choice is being made through accumulating economic constraint rather than deliberate cultural decision.

The documented pattern of congressional trading that has statistically outperformed market benchmarks is not a partisan issue. Academic research covering members of both parties has found returns inconsistent with ordinary investment skill or random chance. The structural explanation is available in public disclosure records: members of Congress sit on the committees that write the legislation affecting the industries in which they hold personal investments. This is not a conflict of interest that occasionally produces problematic outcomes. It is a conflict of interest that is the routine operating condition of the legislative process.

The STOCK Act was passed in 2012 specifically to prevent trading on non-public legislative information. Its average fine for disclosure violations is $218. It has produced essentially zero meaningful prosecutions in its history. The bipartisan resistance to strengthening it — which would require members of both parties to forgo a documented source of above-market returns — explains its toothlessness more completely than any other factor.

The Federal Reserve's quantitative easing programs — $4+ trillion in asset purchases between 2008 and 2022 — inflated equity and real estate prices. The top 1% of households own approximately 54% of all publicly traded equity. The bottom 50% own approximately 1%. When QE inflated asset prices, it primarily transferred wealth to the top 10% of households. When it ended and rates rose, the same households most exposed to rising mortgage rates — those with variable rate debt or those priced out of ownership during the QE period — bore the primary cost. This is not a conspiracy. It is the documented mechanical consequence of using asset price inflation as the primary monetary transmission mechanism in an economy with extreme wealth concentration.

The downstream consequence is a population that receives information optimized for its emotional activation rather than its accuracy — and then cannot form the shared factual baseline from which collective problem-solving would need to proceed.

  • Without social capital, every crisis must route through centralized institutions — the same ones with 2% public confidence ratings
  • This creates a trap: the decentralized response infrastructure has dissolved, and the centralized replacement has lost the trust required to function

Social capital is the connective tissue that allows communities to respond to crises without routing everything through centralized institutions. It is built through repeated face-to-face interaction, shared purpose, mutual obligation, and trust developed over time in the context of specific relationships. Robert Putnam documented its collapse in Bowling Alone, published in 2000. That collapse has continued and accelerated in the twenty-six years since. The civic associations, fraternal organizations, labor unions, neighborhood groups, and service clubs that once provided the infrastructure for decentralized collective action have hollowed out across nearly every community type.

Religious institutions deserve special treatment here — not as a theological matter but as a social infrastructure matter. For most of American history, religious congregations were the primary providers of community belonging, mutual aid networks, shared moral frameworks, and the regular face-to-face interaction that builds trust between neighbors. No government program has successfully replicated any of these functions at scale. The decline of religious participation below 50% for the first time in American history does not represent freedom from something. It represents the removal of documented social infrastructure whose absence has measurable wellbeing consequences — documented in the Gallup data on social support as a wellbeing predictor and in the Surgeon General's declaration of loneliness as a public health epidemic.

The structural trap this creates is significant: the crises documented in this index require collective response. Collective response requires either social capital — the decentralized community infrastructure that allows people to organize and act together — or institutional trust — the confidence that centralized institutions will act competently in the public interest. This index documents that social capital has collapsed and institutional trust has collapsed simultaneously. The result is a population that can accurately diagnose its situation but lacks both the decentralized infrastructure and the institutional confidence to address it.

The AI displacement crisis has two dimensions that must be assessed separately. The first is economic — the documented and accelerating elimination of roles, the disruption of the career ladder at its entry point, the specific vulnerability of credentialed white-collar workers in exactly the roles AI targets first. The second is existential — the question of what human beings are for in an economy where machine capability is expanding faster than the cultural, educational, and political systems can process.

The economic dimension is measurable. 55,000 jobs directly eliminated by AI in 2025 represents a 12-fold increase from 2023. Software developers aged 22–25 experienced a nearly 20% employment decline from their 2022 peak. The career ladder is being severed at its base — without entry-level roles providing foundational experience, the pipeline through which workers develop the expertise to fill senior roles is disrupted. Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has stated publicly that AI could eliminate half of all entry-level white-collar jobs within one to four years. The IMF estimates 60% of jobs in advanced economies are already exposed to AI. These are not theoretical projections from people hostile to the technology. They are assessments from people building it.

The existential dimension requires honest assessment rather than techno-optimist reassurance. Work provides not just income but structure, identity, social belonging, and a sense of competence and contribution. Viktor Frankl's clinical observation — that human beings can endure almost any suffering if they understand its meaning, but cannot endure purposelessness — describes the specific character of the AI threat with precision. The fear driving the displacement anxiety numbers is not primarily fear of poverty. It is fear of a world in which human effort and human contribution are structurally unnecessary. Psychiatrists at the University of Florida have proposed a formal clinical construct — Artificial Intelligence Replacement Dysfunction (AIRD) — to describe the psychological and existential distress of individuals facing this threat. That a clinical community is proposing new diagnostic categories for this experience is itself an indicator of its scale and seriousness.

The opioid and fentanyl crisis is primarily an institutional failure story. The pharmaceutical chapter is documented in court proceedings from the Purdue Pharma litigation, congressional testimony, and investigative reporting: Purdue Pharma's own internal documents show the company knew OxyContin's addiction risk was significantly higher than represented to prescribers and regulators. The FDA approval process failed to catch or act on this. The distribution chain — pharmaceutical distributors who shipped hundreds of millions of pills to communities whose pharmacies could not possibly have had legitimate patients to receive them — operated within a regulatory framework that did not treat volume as a warning sign until the death toll was undeniable.

The Sackler family extracted approximately $11 billion from Purdue Pharma before the company entered bankruptcy in 2019. The bankruptcy proceedings and subsequent settlement — which shielded family members from personal liability in exchange for a payment that represented a fraction of what was extracted — is itself a documented case of the legal system treating financial liability differently for those with sufficient resources to navigate it. The financial beneficiaries of the crisis that killed 500,000 Americans emerged largely intact. This is part of the institutional pattern that explains the 2% confidence number.

The community-level consequences compound every other domain in this index. The communities most devastated by the opioid crisis experienced permanent damage to workforce participation — a documented reduction in labor force participation that predates and extends beyond the acute overdose statistics. Family formation collapsed in affected communities. Civic capacity was destroyed. These are not recoverable in the short term regardless of what policy changes occur. They represent a permanent subtraction from the social and economic capacity of hundreds of communities that has never been honestly accounted for in the public discourse about the crisis.

Infrastructure decay is the physical manifestation of a political system that systematically discounts long-term collective investment in favor of short-term political incentives. Roads, bridges, water systems, the electrical grid, and wastewater treatment do not produce ribbon-cutting moments on the maintenance schedule. They produce catastrophic failures — collapses, contamination events, cascading blackouts — when the maintenance that was deferred long enough finally comes due. The political incentive structure systematically prefers the ribbon-cutting to the maintenance until the failure becomes unavoidable.

The American Society of Civil Engineers' infrastructure report card assigns a C− to US infrastructure overall. Over 42,000 bridges are rated structurally deficient — not hypothetically at risk but currently rated as having significant deterioration of structural components. Millions of Americans are served by water systems with lead infrastructure — a category of contamination whose neurological effects on children are documented, permanent, and severe. The Flint crisis was not an anomaly. It was a visible instance of a condition affecting water systems across hundreds of communities whose failures were less dramatic and therefore less reported.

The fiscal interaction is direct and unresolvable under current trajectories: the infrastructure deficit requires $2.6 trillion in investment over 10 years according to ASCE. The national debt trajectory documented in Domain IV — $1 trillion in annual interest payments growing to $2.1 trillion by 2036 — removes the fiscal space that would be required to address this deficit. The political will to raise revenues or cut other spending at the scale required does not exist in either party. The physical infrastructure of the country will therefore continue to deteriorate at a pace determined by the rate at which current facilities fail, not by any rational maintenance or investment plan.
